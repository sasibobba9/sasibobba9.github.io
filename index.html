Intro

AIM OF PROJECT

The aim of this project is to design and develop a bio-inspired robotic model of a manta ray that replicates the natural form and smooth fin movements of the real-life marine creature. The main objective is to combine nature-inspired design with modern engineering techniques to create both a functional robotic prototype and a smaller toy version of a manta ray. As a group, we began this project by brainstorming ideas and researching various marine creatures. We finalised the manta ray concept because of its unique shape, elegant movement, and potential for creative mechanical design. We started developing sketches and explored different possibilities for creating a toy manta ray with movable fins. To achieve accuracy in design, we used 3D scanning hardware and software to capture detailed anatomical features from existing manta ray models. Motion capture technology further helped us study and understand the natural fin movement, allowing us to replicate it in our design. The design and modelling process was carried out using Computer-Aided Design (CAD) software and 3D modelling tools. Haptic input devices and sculpting software enabled us to fine-tune the complex organic structure of the manta ray with better control and precision. Finally, we used 3D printing technology and resin production to fabricate the parts and assemble the prototype. The toy version features articulated fins designed to move naturally, serving as both an educational tool and a demonstration of biomimicry and mechanical articulation. This project demonstrates how nature can inspire innovative engineering solutions through advanced design, motion analysis, and modern fabrication techniques..

PROJECT SPECIFICATIONS

This project focuses on designing and developing a bio-inspired robotic model of a manta ray using university-provided technologies such as 3D scanning, 3D printing, and digital manufacturing tools. The main objective is to create a lifelike robotic prototype that closely replicates the natural shape and smooth fin movements of a real manta ray. The design process involved using 3D scanning hardware and software to capture accurate anatomical details of the manta ray. We used Onshape platform, a cloud-based 3D modelling platform, to develop and refine the digital design of the model, ensuring precision in structure and movement design. The final prototype was fabricated using 3D printing and resin production techniques available in the university's workshop facilities. This project highlights how combining biomimicry, mechanical design, and advanced digital manufacturing methods can lead to innovative solutions in underwater robotics, offering potential applications in research, exploration, and educational demonstrations.

ARTIFACT

The final artefact is a 3D-printed manta ray model designed to replicate the natural shape and smooth fin movements of a real manta ray. The model was created using PLA material and carefully designed to capture the detailed structure of the marine creature. The fins of the manta ray feature a flexible 3D-printed design that allows them to move in a lifelike manner, closely mimicking the gentle, flowing motion of real manta rays. This artefact demonstrates the use of biomimicry and 3D printing technologies to create a functional and visually accurate representation of aquatic movement in robotics.

Methods

The development of the manta ray model involved multiple design techniques and advanced technologies to ensure accuracy, realistic texture, and movement:
  • 3D scanning hardware and software were used not only to capture the overall shape of the manta ray but also to extract detailed skin texture patterns from existing fish models. This helped in adding a realistic surface finish to the final design.
  • Onshape (3D modelling software) was used to digitally design and develop the manta ray model structure, focusing on achieving flexibility in fin design and maintaining anatomical accuracy.
  • Haptic input devices and 3D sculpting software allowed for fine-tuning the organic shapes and adding surface details with precision.
  • 3D printing technology (FDM) was used to fabricate all parts of the manta ray using PLA material in the university’s workshop facilities.
  • Resin production techniques were applied for surface finishing, improving durability, and enhancing the aesthetic appearance of the model.

By combining these methods, the project successfully developed a bio-inspired manta ray model that not only captures realistic movement but also reflects the fine surface textures found in nature.

Materials

  • Poly lactic Acid (PLA) Filament – Used for 3D printing the entire structure of the manta ray, including the body and fins, due to its lightweight and durable properties.
  • Pins – Used for assembling and connecting the movable fin parts to the main body, allowing flexible motion.
  • Basic Electronics – Servo motors,jumper wiress, raspberry pi, Switch were used to create controlled and smooth fin movement, replicating the natural motion of a real manta ray.
  • * Fishing Thread – Used to connect the fins with the servo motors, enabling the transfer of motion from the motors to the fins in a lightweight and flexible manner, supporting natural articulation.

3D Printed Parts

  • Hollow main body shell replicating the natural shape of a manta ray
  • Segmented left and right pectoral fins for flexible movement
  • Tail and caudal fin with optional motion capability
  • Internal mounting structure to support servo motors and pin connections for fin articulation

CONCEPT

Development Process

  1. Research and Conceptualization:
    The project began with extensive research into the biology, movement, and structure of real manta rays. This included studying fin motion, body proportions, and underwater dynamics. Sketches and rough models were created to explore the overall shape and articulation points.
  2. 3D Scanning and Reference Collection:
    For anatomical accuracy, reference images and 3D scans (where available) were analyzed to better understand the curvature and surface details of the manta ray's body and fins. This helped in guiding the CAD modeling phase.
  3. CAD Design and Modeling:
    Using Fusion 360, the manta ray model was created in multiple segments to allow for articulation, especially in the fins. The design incorporated joints and connection points that would allow motion, either mechanically or via motorized control for future upgrades.
  4. Simulation and Motion Planning:
    Basic motion simulations were run within Fusion 360 to verify the range of movement for the fins and ensure smooth mechanical interaction. Design iterations were carried out to minimize friction and optimize motion paths.
  5. Material Selection:
    PLA was selected as the primary material due to its rigidity and ease of printing. TPU was considered for the fins to allow flexibility, depending on the specific part function. Threaded rods and connectors were planned for assembling moving parts.
  6. 3D Printing:
    The model was split into separate printable parts: the main body, left and right fins, and optional tail section. Each part was sliced using appropriate settings in Cura for PLA. Supports and orientation were carefully managed to reduce print failures.
  7. Post-Processing and Assembly:
    After printing, supports were removed and the surfaces were cleaned using sanding tools. The parts were assembled using pins and joints designed into the CAD model. The articulation was tested and adjusted for smoother motion.
  8. Final Testing and Presentation:
    The assembled manta ray model was evaluated for its visual resemblance and mechanical motion. Demonstration videos and photographs were taken to document the process. The final prototype was showcased as both a functional toy and a robotic concept model.

DESIGN

Design Resources and Technologies Used

The design and fabrication of the manta ray-inspired robotic model utilized a wide range of advanced digital and physical tools, workshop facilities, and prototyping technologies. Each stage of the design process was supported by specialized equipment and software to ensure precision, creativity, and functionality in the final output. Below is an overview of the key resources involved:

  1. 3D Scanning Hardware, Software, and Workshop Facilities:
    High-resolution 3D scanning equipment was employed to capture reference shapes and textures from natural or modeled forms. The scanning process helped guide the anatomical accuracy of the manta ray's body structure. Dedicated scanning workspaces ensured proper lighting and stability for accurate model acquisition.
  2. Motion Capture Hardware, Software, and Stage Facilities:
    Motion capture technology was used to study and replicate the fluid movements of manta ray fins. A motion capture stage setup allowed for the recording of fin-like movements, which were later mapped into the digital model to assist in simulating and designing articulated motion in the fins.
  3. Computer-Aided Design (CAD) Software Package:
    Fusion 360 was utilized for creating detailed mechanical designs of the manta ray model. This included segmenting the fins for movement, planning joints and pin holes, and validating tolerances for 3D printed assembly. The CAD environment allowed for parametric modeling and simulation of the final structure.
  4. 3D Modelling Software:
    In addition to CAD, organic modeling tools were used to sculpt the outer body for aesthetic and biomimetic realism. This supported the creation of a smooth and flowing manta ray shape, closely mimicking natural forms beyond purely mechanical components.
  5. Haptic Input Hardware and 3D Sculpting Software:
    Haptic feedback tools were used to interactively sculpt portions of the manta ray’s outer skin, enabling more intuitive and tactile control over digital shaping. This approach allowed for enhanced surface detailing and ergonomic refinement in the body design.
  6. 3D Printing Hardware and Workshop Facilities:
    Multiple components of the model were fabricated using Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D printers. PLA was primarily used due to its accessibility and rigidity, with workshop facilities providing support for multi-material printing, surface finishing, and part testing.
  7. Laser Cutting Hardware and Workshop Facilities:
    For internal frame structures or precise mounting plates, laser cutting was employed on acrylic or wood sheets. Laser-cutting helped produce accurate 2D profiles that supported the assembly of more complex 3D components.
  8. Resin Production, Post-Production, and Composite Workshop Facilities:
    Where high-detail or smooth finishes were required (such as skin overlays or ornamental features), resin-based techniques were explored. Post-processing stations allowed for curing, sanding, and painting of resin parts. Additionally, composite workshops offered opportunities to explore hybrid structures and reinforce delicate joints.

FINAL-DESIGN

Final Prototype Overview

The final design of the manta ray-inspired robotic model demonstrates a successful translation of biological inspiration into a functional and visually engaging artifact. The model integrates flexible pectoral fins attached to a rigid central body, enabling lifelike undulating motion through articulated joints.

Features

  • Realistic anatomical structure based on 3D scanning and biological references
  • Modular fin segments for smooth mechanical movement
  • Lightweight PLA and TPU parts designed for easy assembly and user interaction
  • Hollow core with internal space for servo mounting or electronics
  • Toy-scale version with manually movable fins for educational use

Presentation and Testing

The assembled model was showcased as both a design prototype and a proof-of-concept for educational and bio-robotic applications. Manual movement tests confirmed the effective articulation of fins, mimicking aquatic gliding behavior. The toy-scale version was durable and interactive, suitable for demonstration in outreach or classroom settings.

Conclusion

This project successfully explored the fusion of biomimicry and engineering through the development of a manta ray-inspired robotic model. By utilizing a range of modern fabrication tools—including CAD software, 3D printing, and motion analysis—an anatomically accurate and functional design was achieved.

The project not only met its aim of replicating the form and motion of a manta ray but also demonstrated the educational and design potential of robotics inspired by nature. Future enhancements may include full electronic integration, water testing, and surface texture development to further enhance realism and functionality.
